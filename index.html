Key takeaway For a growing multi-property portfolio, a discretionary family trust generally wins on asset protection, flexibility, and tax planning — but the lending disadvantage (no negative gearing pass-through) is the single biggest practical hurdle that stops many investors.

The negative gearing trade-off In early years with high interest costs and low rent, properties often run at a loss. Individuals claim that loss against salary immediately. A trust traps the loss — it carries forward until the trust is net positive. This is the #1 reason some investors stay in personal names early, then consider a trust once the portfolio is cashflow-positive.

Click a scenario to see the recommendation.

Young couple, first investment property

Both on PAYG income. High LVR needed. Property likely negatively geared for 3-5 years.

Individual names recommended
Negative gearing benefits are immediate and meaningful on PAYG income. Trust setup costs aren't justified for one property. Simpler lending. Revisit when portfolio reaches 2-3 properties and cashflow turns positive.

Growing portfolio (3-5 properties), one high earner

Portfolio transitioning to cashflow-positive. Income disparity between partners. Long-term hold strategy.

Trust recommended
Income distribution flexibility becomes very valuable with income disparity. Asset protection scales with portfolio size. Land tax surcharge is a cost but is offset by tax savings from income splitting. The negative gearing loss is minimal once properties are cashflow-neutral or positive.

Business owners with liability exposure

One or both partners run a business. Potential for litigation or creditor claims. Properties are wealth preservation.

Trust strongly recommended
Asset protection is the primary driver. A properly structured discretionary trust with a corporate trustee provides the strongest barrier between business liabilities and the property portfolio. This alone can justify the ongoing costs.

Older couple, estate planning focus

Properties are the main wealth asset. Concerned about smooth transfer to children. Want to avoid probate.

Trust recommended
Trust assets don't go through probate — no delays, no public record, no estate challenges. Appointor/guardian roles transfer smoothly. Can bring children in as beneficiaries gradually. No stamp duty on change of beneficiary. Watch the vesting date — ensure it extends far enough.

Couple with adult children earning low income

Children are students, starting careers, or at home. Parents want to minimise family tax bill.

Trust recommended
Distributing rental income and capital gains to low-income adult children (18+) is the classic trust play. Must be genuine — child is a beneficiary, receives real distribution, must declare it. Section 100A risk if parents retain the cash and child "lends" it back.

High-growth area, plan to sell within 5 years

Buy-renovate-sell strategy. Capital gains are the primary return. Multiple flips planned.

Depends on income levels
Trust lets you direct capital gains to the lowest-taxed beneficiary at sale time — massive advantage. But if held <12 months, no 50% discount for anyone. ATO may also treat frequent flips as business income (no CGT discount, assessed as ordinary income) — trust won't help with that classification. Get advice on the revenue vs capital account distinction.

Bottom line for most multi-property couples Start in personal names for the first 1–2 negatively geared properties. Once the portfolio turns cashflow-positive (or you have adult children to distribute to), establish a trust for new acquisitions. Transferring existing properties into a trust triggers stamp duty + CGT — so plan the structure before you buy, not after.

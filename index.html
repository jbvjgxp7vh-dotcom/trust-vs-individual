The Capacity Trade — Not Free, But Worth It

Putting #1 in Mai's individual name shifts ~$220K of exposure off Tim onto Mai compared to all-trust. Mai's exposure rises from $2.18M (all-trust) to $2.40M (locked); Tim's drops from $2.18M to $1.97M. Total portfolio debt is identical at $4.37M — the structure just decides who carries it.

This is a concentrated trade, not a free option. We're spending $220K of Mai's headroom (cutting her buffer from $320K to $100K against the placeholder $2.5M ceiling) to buy three things:

• ~$10–15K NPV timing benefit on the grandfathered PAYG offset (cash received year-by-year vs deferred trust shield used years ~8–12; total losses preserved either way)

• $10K LMI waiver + ~$10–27K rate edge over the loan life

• $530K of Tim's reserve capacity — enough for an 11th deal, an equity-release event, or an emergency buffer

The trade-off is favourable: ~$30–50K NPV cash advantage + the optionality of Tim's reserve, in exchange for tightening the limiting party (Mai) by $220K within a ceiling that still completes the plan. Earlier framings overstated this as "$55K locked-in" — the corrected version (timing-shifted, not forfeited) is smaller but still wins.

2026–27 Federal Budget Changes 12 May 2026

1. Negative Gearing Quarantine (from 1 July 2027)

Properties bought after 7:30pm 12 May 2026: rental losses on existing residential properties are quarantined — they can only offset other residential property income (including capital gains), not PAYG salary.

Applies to individuals, partnerships, companies AND trusts. New builds exempt.

McPherson is grandfathered (contract 7 May). All future existing-dwelling purchases are affected.

2. 30% Minimum Tax on Discretionary Trusts (from 1 July 2028)

Trustees pay 30% minimum tax on trust taxable income. Beneficiaries get a non-refundable credit.

Impact on Tim & Mai: Both on 45% marginal rate — the 30% minimum is below your marginal rate, so no extra tax. The credit covers the 30% and you top up to 45% on your personal return.

What it kills: income splitting to low-income beneficiaries (<$45K income). Streaming to kids or non-working family members no longer saves tax.

What This Means for Future Properties

The main advantage of individual ownership (negative gearing against salary) is gone for all future purchases. This levels the playing field between individual and trust — the trust's structural benefits (asset protection, distribution flexibility) now come at a lower relative cost.

Strategic Priority & Capital Cadence Decision Frame

The structure decision is made under a scale-first priority. A ~$7.7K/yr holding-cost difference on a single property is acceptable if it preserves the borrowing runway to acquire more. A structure that minimises holding cost but caps the portfolio at 4–5 properties is the worse outcome.

This flips the default trade-off: where the doc earlier described the trust premium as "the price of preserving borrowing runway," that price is now what we are explicitly willing to pay.

Multi-Lender by Necessity, Not Choice

The locked plan cannot be executed through a single lender. Three independent reasons force a 2–3 lender rotation across the portfolio.

1. Per-lender exposure caps

Most second-tier lenders cap total exposure to one borrower at ~$3–5M. The locked plan totals $4.37M of debt — well into the cap zone for Macquarie/Firstmac and likely over for Liberty/Pepper.

Even if Macquarie wrote everything from a policy standpoint, they'd cut you off before #10.

2. Servicing buffers compound against you

Each lender assesses their own existing loans at their own inflated buffer rate (typically actual + 3%). Stay with one lender → every new application uses the inflated rate against all your prior loans with them.

A different lender often assesses your existing loans at actual repayment level (or close to it), unlocking servicing capacity that Macquarie's own calculator says you don't have.

3. Concentration risk

10 properties through one lender = single point of failure. Policy change, valuation dispute, or relationship breakdown affects the whole portfolio simultaneously.

2–3 lenders means a Macquarie tightening doesn't freeze acquisitions or refinances — Firstmac/Liberty fill the gap.

Practical Rotation

Target: 2–3 lenders across the 9 trust loans. The Tier 1 → Tier 2 → Tier 3 progression below isn't a fallback list — it's the actual deployment sequence:

• Macquarie writes #2–#4 (~$1.3M against Macquarie's exposure cap)

• Firstmac writes #5–#7 (~$1.3M against a fresh ceiling, with Macquarie's existing debt assessed at actual rates)

• Liberty or Pepper writes #8–#10 (rate premium accepted; specialist servicing flexibility unlocks the back half)

Cross-collateralisation stays off — every property is standalone security against its specific loan, regardless of which lender wrote it.

Caveats Before Acting on This Ranking

• This ranking reflects general industry positioning, not live mid-2026 broker data — Jenny / her successor must validate before #2 finance application

• Policies change quarterly; a Tier 1 lender today can drop tiers overnight (Bankwest is the cautionary tale)

• Trust structure (discretionary vs unit), guarantor relationship, and actual income distribution patterns all shift treatment

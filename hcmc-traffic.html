Roads Can't Keep Up

Only ~2% of HCMC's land is roads, vs 20-25% in Tokyo or Paris.

The city's French-colonial street grid was designed for 1.5 million people on bicycles and cyclos. Today's 10M+ population overwhelms corridors that haven't widened since the 1960s. New road projects average 10-15 years from approval to completion due to land acquisition challenges.

No Metro (Until Now)

Metro Line 1 has been under construction since 2012. Without rail, every trip is on the road.

Originally due in 2018, Line 1 connects Ben Thanh Market to Suoi Tien (19.7 km, 14 stations). Cost overruns pushed the budget from $1.1B to $2.7B. Without it, the city has zero high-capacity rapid transit, forcing all 26M+ daily trips onto surface roads.

Flooding & Geography

River delta, barely above sea level. Monsoon floods cut road capacity in half.

HCMC sits just 0.5-1.5m above sea level. Climate change and upstream dam operations have intensified tidal flooding. Nguyen Huu Canh Street near Saigon River floods almost weekly during rainy season, creating cascading gridlock across Binh Thanh and District 1.

Sprawl Without Transit

Housing pushed outward but jobs stayed central. No express transit to connect them.

Districts like Binh Chanh, Hoc Mon, and Cu Chi grew 5-8% annually as affordable housing moved outward. But employment remains concentrated in Districts 1, 3, and Thu Duc. Workers commute 15-25 km each way on two-lane roads never designed for that volume.

🚗

Car Ownership Rising Fast

Cars take 4-6x more road space per person. Even modest mode shift worsens gridlock.

Vietnam's middle class is the fastest-growing in SE Asia. Car registrations in HCMC grew ~18% annually from 2015-2023. A single car replacing 3 motorbikes actually uses more road space. The road network literally cannot absorb this transition without mass transit alternatives.

🛑

Weak Traffic Management

Manual signals, loose enforcement. Motorbikes, cars, and trucks all share the same lanes.

Only ~30% of intersections have adaptive signal timing. Lane markings are treated as suggestions. Right-of-way defaults to whoever is bigger or faster. Bus lanes exist on paper but are routinely blocked by parked motorbikes. Enforcement cameras are increasing but coverage remains thin.

How It Got Here

Pre-1975

Saigon's French colonial grid served ~1.5 million. Wide boulevards like Nguyen Hue were built for pedestrians and cyclos.

1986 — Doi Moi

Economic reforms opened markets. Honda, Yamaha, and local brands flooded the country with affordable motorbikes. Personal mobility exploded.

2000s — Construction Boom

High-rises multiplied without proportional road or transit investment. Population density surged in Districts 1, 3, Binh Thanh, and Thu Duc.

2010s — Car Growth Begins

Vietnam's growing middle class started buying cars. Registrations climbed 15-20% per year. Roads built for motorbikes couldn't absorb larger vehicles.

2020s — Metro Delayed, Ride-Hail Adds Volume

Grab added millions of car trips. Metro Line 1 missed deadline after deadline. Thu Duc City merger created a mega-district with no transit spine.

The Cultural Factor

Vietnamese traffic operates on a negotiation model, not a rules model. Riders signal intent through micro-movements, horn taps, and eye contact. Intersections that look like pure chaos actually self-organise through thousands of individual negotiations per minute.

This system is remarkably efficient at low speeds — often more throughput per lane than signalised intersections in Western cities.

The motorbike isn't just transport. It's a mobile shop counter, a family vehicle (3-4 riders is normal), a delivery truck, and a social space. Vietnamese cities were literally redesigned around the motorbike — narrow alleys (hems) that only a two-wheeler can navigate form entire neighbourhoods.

Proposing to ban motorbikes is like proposing to ban walking — it would disconnect millions from daily life.

The negotiation system breaks down when cars enter the mix. Cars are too large, too enclosed, and too rigid in their lane requirements to participate in the micro-negotiation dance. A single car disrupts the flow that dozens of motorbikes maintained smoothly.

This is the core tension: rising incomes push people toward cars, but every car makes the system worse for everyone, including the driver.

Ho Chi Minh City: 0 metro lines operational. Primary mode: motorbike (~75%). Peak centre speed: 10-15 km/h. Population density: ~4,300/km². The city with the least road infrastructure and the most vehicles per capita in this comparison.

Bangkok: 5+ rail lines (BTS, MRT, ARL). Mixed car/transit mode split. Peak centre speed: 15-20 km/h. Road share 5x higher than HCMC but still heavily congested due to car dominance and sprawl. Rail investment since 1999 has eased some corridors.

Jakarta: 1 MRT line + commuter rail. Car/motorbike mix. Peak centre speed: 5-10 km/h — often worse than HCMC. Similar low road-share problem. TransJakarta BRT carries 1M+ daily but can't offset car growth.

Tokyo: 13 metro lines + extensive JR/private rail. Rail carries ~60% of commuters. Peak centre speed: 25-30 km/h. The benchmark: 24% road share + world-class transit = a functional megacity. HCMC needs 50 years of this kind of investment.

What's Being Done

Current & Planned Interventions

  • Metro Line 1 (Ben Thanh — Suoi Tien): 19.7 km, 14 stations, nearing completion
  • Metro Line 2 (Ben Thanh — Tham Luong): approved, construction underway
  • BRT Line 1 along Vo Van Kiet — Pham Hung corridor
  • Motorbike ban proposed for central districts by 2030
  • Ring Road 3 expressway to relieve cross-city truck traffic
  • Smart traffic signal pilots in Districts 1 and 10
  • Thu Duc City transit-oriented development plans

The fundamental challenge: HCMC needs to build 50 years of transit infrastructure while the city continues to grow at 3% per year. Every year of delay means more vehicles on roads that were never built for this volume.
